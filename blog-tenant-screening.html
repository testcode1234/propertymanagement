The difference between a good tenant and a problem tenant can cost you tens of thousands of dollars. Quality tenant screening is your first and best defense against financial losses from non-payment, property damage, and costly evictions.

The True Cost of a Bad Tenant

Consider what a problem tenant can cost:

  • Lost rent during non-payment: 3-6 months at $2,500/month = $7,500-$15,000
  • Legal fees for eviction: $2,500-$5,000
  • Property damage beyond deposit: $3,000-$10,000+
  • Turnover costs: $2,000-$4,000
  • Your time and stress: Immeasurable

Total potential cost: $15,000-$35,000 or more

Some landlords try to save $30-50 on screening fees, rushing to fill vacancies with minimal vetting. This can lead to losses 500-1000 times greater than the screening cost they tried to save.

Components of Comprehensive Screening

1. Credit Report Analysis

Credit history reveals payment patterns and financial responsibility. We look for:

  • Payment history on debts
  • Outstanding collections or judgments
  • Bankruptcies or foreclosures
  • Credit utilization and overall score

2. Criminal Background Check

While fair housing laws limit how you can use criminal history, serious convictions (especially recent ones related to violence, property damage, or drug trafficking) warrant careful consideration.

3. Eviction History Search

Previous evictions are often the strongest predictor of future problems. National eviction databases help identify serial problem tenants.

4. Employment & Income Verification

We verify employment directly with employers and confirm income meets requirements (typically 3x monthly rent). Pay stubs alone aren't enough‚Äîthey can be forged.

5. Previous Landlord References

Current landlords may give positive references to problem tenants just to get rid of them. That's why we contact previous landlords (going back 2-3 rentals) for honest feedback on:

  • Payment history
  • Property condition
  • Lease violations
  • Neighbor complaints
  • Would they rent to this person again?

Red Flags We Watch For

  • Inconsistencies in application information
  • Evasive answers about previous rentals or employment
  • Pressure to skip screening steps or rush the process
  • Insufficient income relative to rent
  • Multiple recent evictions or judgments
  • Negative references from previous landlords
  • Inability to provide required documentation

üí° The Value of Experience

Professional property managers have screened thousands of applicants. We know which red flags are deal-breakers, which warrant additional investigation, and how to evaluate borderline cases. This experience is invaluable in making good tenant selections.

Fair Housing Compliance

Screening must comply with fair housing laws. You cannot discriminate based on protected characteristics including race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, disability, or in California, source of income.

Best practices for compliance:

  • Use consistent, objective screening criteria for all applicants
  • Base decisions on verifiable financial and rental history facts
  • Document your decision-making process
  • Provide adverse action notices when required
  • Make reasonable accommodations for disabilities
