2025 California Landlord-Tenant Law Updates

üìÖ December 2025 ‚è±Ô∏è 6 min read ‚öñÔ∏è Legal & Compliance

California's rental housing laws continue to evolve, with 2025 bringing several important updates that property owners and managers must understand and implement. Whether you're a seasoned landlord or new to rental property ownership, staying compliant with state and local regulations is essential to protect your investment and avoid costly penalties.

This guide covers the key legal changes taking effect in 2025 and what they mean for your rental property operations.

Important Legal Disclaimer

This article provides general information about California landlord-tenant laws but is not legal advice. Laws can vary by locality and individual circumstances. Always consult with a qualified attorney for specific legal guidance regarding your rental property.

AB 1482: Rent Control and Just Cause Eviction

The Tenant Protection Act of 2019 (AB 1482) remains in effect and continues to impact most rental properties in California. Here's what you need to know about the current requirements:

Rent Increase Limitations

Under AB 1482, rent increases are capped at the lower of:

  • 5% plus the local Consumer Price Index (CPI), or
  • 10% total

For 2025, with inflation moderating, most areas in California will see maximum allowable increases in the 6-8% range. However, this is a maximum‚Äînot a recommendation. Market conditions and tenant retention strategies should guide your actual rent increase decisions.

Properties Exempt from AB 1482

Certain properties remain exempt from these rent control provisions:

  • Housing built within the last 15 years (currently properties built after 2010)
  • Single-family homes and condos (unless owned by corporations or LLCs owning more than two properties)
  • Deed-restricted affordable housing

Just Cause Eviction Requirements

AB 1482 also requires "just cause" to terminate tenancies after a tenant has been in residence for 12 months. This means you must have a valid reason‚Äîsuch as non-payment of rent, lease violations, or owner move-in‚Äîto end a tenancy. Simply wanting to increase rent beyond allowed limits or re-rent to someone else is not sufficient cause.

Security Deposit Regulations

California maintains strict rules regarding security deposits, and compliance is essential to avoid disputes and potential penalties.

Maximum Security Deposit Amounts

The maximum security deposit you can collect is:

  • Unfurnished properties: Two months' rent (for tenancies starting before July 1, 2024) or one month's rent (for tenancies starting after July 1, 2024)
  • Furnished properties: Three months' rent (before July 1, 2024) or two months' rent (after July 1, 2024)
  • Service members: No more than one month's rent regardless of when the tenancy began

Security Deposit Return Requirements

Landlords have 21 days after a tenant moves out to:

  1. Return the full security deposit, or
  2. Provide an itemized statement of deductions with remaining funds

Failure to meet this deadline can result in penalties, including potentially forfeiting your right to retain any portion of the deposit.

Habitability and Maintenance Requirements

California's implied warranty of habitability requires landlords to maintain rental properties in livable condition. Recent emphasis on enforcement means compliance is more critical than ever.

Essential Requirements

Your rental property must provide:

  • Effective waterproofing and weather protection
  • Plumbing facilities in good working order
  • Hot and cold running water
  • Heating facilities in good working order
  • Electrical lighting with safe wiring
  • Clean and sanitary conditions (including pest control)
  • Adequate trash receptacles
  • Working locks on doors and windows
  • Properly installed smoke and carbon monoxide detectors

Response Time Requirements

While California law doesn't specify exact timeframes for repairs (except for emergency situations), courts generally expect:

  • Emergency repairs (no heat, major leaks, gas leaks): Immediate to 24 hours
  • Urgent repairs (broken toilets, no hot water): 24-72 hours
  • Non-urgent repairs: Within 30 days

Failure to maintain habitable conditions can result in tenants withholding rent, "repair and deduct," or legal action for damages.

Fair Housing Compliance

Fair housing laws protect tenants from discrimination based on protected characteristics. California offers broader protections than federal law, prohibiting discrimination based on:

  • Race or color
  • National origin
  • Religion
  • Sex (including sexual harassment)
  • Familial status (families with children)
  • Disability or medical condition
  • Genetic information
  • Marital status
  • Sexual orientation and gender identity
  • Source of income (including Section 8 vouchers)
  • Age
  • Ancestry

Source of Income Protection

California's source of income protection means you cannot refuse to rent to someone simply because they use a housing voucher or receive public assistance. Violations can result in significant penalties and legal action.

Required Disclosures

California requires landlords to provide various disclosures to tenants. Failure to provide these can result in penalties or liability:

Move-In Disclosures

  • Lead-based paint disclosure (for properties built before 1978)
  • Mold disclosure (explaining mold prevention and tenant responsibilities)
  • Bed bug disclosure (history of infestations in the past two years)
  • Presence of smoke detectors and carbon monoxide devices
  • Death in unit disclosure (if death occurred within three years)
  • Methamphetamine contamination (if property was used as a drug lab)
  • Registered sex offender database (information on how to access the database)

Ongoing Disclosures

  • Notice of intent to enter (typically 24 hours, with exceptions for emergencies)
  • Notice of rent increases (30 days for increases less than 10%; 90 days for increases of 10% or more within 12 months)
  • Notice of sale (if property is being sold)

Eviction Process Changes

California's eviction process has become increasingly technical, with strict procedural requirements that must be followed precisely.

Notice Requirements

Different situations require different notice periods:

  • 3-Day Notice: For non-payment of rent or other lease violations
  • 30-Day Notice: For terminating month-to-month tenancies (tenancy less than one year) without cause, where allowed
  • 60-Day Notice: For terminating month-to-month tenancies (tenancy one year or more) without cause, where allowed
  • 90-Day Notice: For terminating subsidized tenancies

Under AB 1482, many "no-cause" evictions are prohibited. Valid reasons for eviction include:

  • Non-payment of rent
  • Breach of lease terms
  • Nuisance or illegal activity
  • Owner move-in (with relocation assistance required)
  • Substantial renovation (with relocation assistance required)
  • Withdrawal from rental market (Ellis Act)

Relocation Assistance

Many California cities require landlords to pay relocation assistance when evicting tenants for owner move-in or major renovations. Amounts vary by locality but can be substantial‚Äîoften equivalent to 2-3 months' rent. Know your local requirements before initiating such evictions.

Local Ordinances

Many California cities have additional rental housing regulations beyond state law. San Diego and surrounding communities have various local requirements:

City of San Diego

  • Additional just cause eviction protections
  • Tenant relocation assistance requirements
  • Rental unit registration in some cases

Other Jurisdictions

Many cities throughout San Diego County have their own ordinances regarding rental housing, rent control, eviction procedures, and tenant protections. Always check local regulations in addition to state law.

Compliance is Essential

California's landlord-tenant laws are among the most tenant-friendly in the nation, with strict requirements and significant penalties for non-compliance. Violations can result in:

  • Financial penalties and fines
  • Tenant lawsuits for damages
  • Loss of eviction cases due to procedural errors
  • Requirement to pay tenant attorney fees
  • Inability to collect rent during disputes
